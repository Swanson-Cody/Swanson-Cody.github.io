Ukulele's Composition

Categories

A ukulele can be broken down into three categories. Each category contains different parts that constitute the ukulele. The categories are: Head, Neck and Body.

Parts

Head

The head contains the tuners. This creates the tautness of the strings. Adjusting the tuners, using the tuning pegs, will alter the sound the instrument makes. (Note that at the bottom of the head we have the nut separating the head and neck which keeps the strings in place. The head is also known as the headstock.)

Neck

The neck contains the frets and fret markers. This is important to help the musician know how to manipulate the sounds of the ukulele by pressing down on different frets/parts of the strings. The top/front of the neck is known as the fretboard.

Body

The sound hole is where the music emits from. The bridge is the anchor for the strings. On the bridge is the saddle which is actually the source of music. When the strings are plucked/strummed it creates energy which causes vibrations in the saddle. This is amplified by the body/chamber and resonated from the sound hole.

Strings

The typical ukulele has four strings. Each string has a label to identify it from the other strings. Knowing the strings helps one to know which to manipulate to create the music you want to hear. From left to right of a ukulele it is: Fourth, Third, Second, First.

Using the tuning pegs, you can adjust the tune to make the instrument play different tunes. The most common tuning for the ukulele is the G C E A. The 4th string is G. The 3rd string is C. the 2nd string is E. The first string is A.

Materials

Ukuleles can be made of plastics, plywoord or laminate woods, or solid hardwoods. Woods used are varied and could range from different types such as mahogany, cedar, acacia koa, spruce, bocote, rosewood, and more.
